# Module: Soteriology

## Scope

The doctrine of salvation (*soteria*) in Christianity: how human beings are reconciled to God, the role of grace and faith, the work of Christ on the cross, and the process by which salvation is applied to individuals and communities. This module focuses on the Western theological tradition (Catholic and Protestant) with pointers to Eastern Orthodox theosis where relevant.

## Core Concepts

- **Atonement** — the means by which Christ's death reconciles humanity to God. Major models include:
  - *Penal substitutionary atonement*: Christ bears the penalty for sin in the sinner's place (Isaiah 53:5; Romans 3:23–25). Developed by the Reformers; systematized in Charles Hodge, *Systematic Theology* (1872), vol. 2.
  - *Christus Victor*: Christ's death and resurrection defeat the powers of sin, death, and the devil (Colossians 2:15). Gustaf Aulen, *Christus Victor* (1931).
  - *Moral influence*: the cross demonstrates God's love and transforms the human heart (1 John 4:10). Peter Abelard, *Commentary on Romans* (c. 1134).
  - *Satisfaction*: sin is an offense against God's honor; Christ's death provides adequate satisfaction. Anselm of Canterbury, *Cur Deus Homo* (1098).
- **Justification** — the act by which God declares the sinner righteous.
  - Protestant: forensic declaration based on imputed righteousness of Christ; received through faith alone (Romans 3:28; Galatians 2:16). Martin Luther, *Lectures on Galatians* (1535).
  - Catholic: an infused transformation involving both faith and cooperation with grace. Council of Trent, Session 6 (1547), Decree on Justification.
  - Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification (1999): Lutheran–Catholic ecumenical convergence on core affirmations.
- **Sanctification** — the progressive transformation of the believer. Debated whether it is entirely God's work (monergism) or involves human cooperation (synergism).
- **Theosis / Divinization** — the Eastern Orthodox emphasis on salvation as participation in the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4). Athanasius: "God became man so that man might become god" (*On the Incarnation*, §54).

## Interpretations

### Reformed (Calvinist)

Salvation is entirely the work of God from election to glorification (the "five points": total depravity, unconditional election, limited atonement, irresistible grace, perseverance of the saints). Human beings contribute nothing to their salvation.

- John Calvin, *Institutes of the Christian Religion* (1559), III.xxi–xxiv.
- Westminster Confession of Faith (1646), chapters IX–XVIII.

### Arminian

God's grace is universal and resistible; salvation depends on the free human response of faith. Election is based on God's foreknowledge of who will believe. Christ's atonement is universal in scope.

- Jacob Arminius, *Declaration of Sentiments* (1608).
- John Wesley, sermon "Free Grace" (1739).

### Catholic

Justification is a real transformation (not merely forensic) involving faith, sacraments, and cooperation with grace. Merit is possible only because of grace; it does not operate independently of God's initiative.

- Council of Trent, Session 6, canons 1–33 on justification.
- Catechism of the Catholic Church §1987–2029.

### Eastern Orthodox

Salvation is understood primarily as theosis — progressive union with God through the Holy Spirit, enabled by the incarnation. Less focused on legal categories of guilt and acquittal; more focused on healing and participation.

- Gregory Palamas, *Triads* (c. 1338): distinction between God's essence and energies.
- Vladimir Lossky, *The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church* (1944).

## Internal Tensions

- Forensic justification (Protestant) vs. transformative justification (Catholic/Orthodox) — is the sinner "declared" or "made" righteous?
- Monergism vs. synergism — does grace operate irresistibly or cooperatively?
- Limited vs. unlimited atonement — did Christ die for the elect only or for all?
- The relationship between justification (one-time event) and sanctification (ongoing process) in Protestant theology: how do they relate without collapsing into each other?
- New Perspective on Paul (E.P. Sanders, N.T. Wright): challenges traditional Protestant readings of Paul on justification; argues "works of the law" refers to Jewish identity markers, not moral effort.

## Current Understanding

Soteriology is the most internally contested locus in Christian systematic theology. The four traditions above represent genuinely distinct soteriological grammars, not merely verbal disagreements. Ecumenical efforts (e.g., the 1999 Joint Declaration) have achieved partial convergence on justification but leave atonement models and the role of sacraments unresolved. Any synthetic account must honestly represent these disagreements rather than prematurely harmonizing them.
