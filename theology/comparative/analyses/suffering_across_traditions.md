# Topic: The Problem of Suffering across Religious Traditions

## Christianity

### Summary

In Christian theology, suffering enters the world through the Fall (Genesis 3) and is bound up with the problem of evil (theodicy). God does not will suffering for its own sake, but permits it within a providential order. The cross of Christ is the central theological response: God enters into human suffering, redeems it, and promises its ultimate elimination in the eschaton (Revelation 21:4, "He will wipe away every tear").

### Key Sources

- Augustine, *City of God* (c. 413–426), Book XII: evil as *privatio boni* (privation of good), not a substance; suffering results from disordered will.
- C.S. Lewis, *The Problem of Pain* (1940): suffering as God's "megaphone to rouse a deaf world" (ch. 6); free will as the necessary condition for both love and suffering.
- Romans 8:18–28 — "I consider that the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed to us."
- Job 38–42 — God's answer from the whirlwind: the mystery of suffering is met not with an explanation but with a theophany.

### Observations (Objective)

- The Bible does not offer a single unified theory of suffering; Job, Ecclesiastes, the Prophets, and Paul each give different framings.
- The cross is presented as both the paradigm of innocent suffering and the means of its redemption.
- Post-Holocaust theology (e.g., Jurgen Moltmann, *The Crucified God*, 1972) insists that any theodicy must reckon with radical, systemic evil, not only individual misfortune.

## Buddhism

### Summary

In Buddhist teaching, suffering (*dukkha*) is the first of the Four Noble Truths and is the defining characteristic of conditioned existence. Dukkha is not a punishment or an anomaly; it is the baseline state of beings trapped in the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (*samsara*). The cause of suffering is craving (*tanha*) and ignorance (*avijja*). The path to the cessation of suffering is the Noble Eightfold Path, culminating in *nibbana* (Pali) / *nirvana* (Sanskrit).

### Key Sources

- *Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta* (SN 56.11) — the Buddha's first sermon setting out the Four Noble Truths.
- Buddhaghosa, *Visuddhimagga* (Path of Purification, 5th c. CE), ch. XVI: detailed analysis of the three characteristics of existence (*tilakkhana*): impermanence (*anicca*), suffering (*dukkha*), non-self (*anatta*).
- Thich Nhat Hanh, *The Heart of the Buddha's Teaching* (1998): accessible modern exposition of the Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path.

### Observations (Objective)

- Buddhism does not frame suffering as a problem requiring external rescue; it is a diagnosis requiring internal transformation.
- There is no creator-God in mainstream Buddhism, so theodicy in the Christian sense does not arise.
- The Mahayana tradition adds the bodhisattva ideal: the enlightened being voluntarily remains in samsara to help others escape suffering (cf. Shantideva, *Bodhicaryavatara*, 8th c. CE).

## Key Differences

| Dimension | Christianity | Buddhism |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Origin of suffering** | The Fall; disordered human will; permitted by a sovereign God | Craving and ignorance within conditioned existence |
| **Nature of the problem** | Moral and relational — separation from God | Existential and cognitive — attachment to impermanent phenomena |
| **Agent of resolution** | God (through Christ's atoning work and the Spirit) | The individual practitioner (through wisdom, ethics, meditation) |
| **End state** | Resurrection, eternal communion with God (eschatological) | Nirvana — cessation of craving and the cycle of rebirth |
| **Role of suffering itself** | Can be redemptive (Romans 5:3–5; 2 Corinthians 12:9) | To be understood and overcome, not valorized |

## Underlying Assumptions

- **Theism vs. non-theism**: Christianity presupposes a personal creator-God whose goodness must be reconciled with the existence of suffering. Buddhism operates without this presupposition, so the "problem" is framed entirely differently.
- **Self**: Christianity assumes a continuous personal soul whose suffering and redemption are meaningful across time. Buddhism denies a permanent self (*anatta*), which reframes what it even means for "someone" to suffer.
- **History**: Christian soteriology is eschatological — suffering will be resolved at the end of history. Buddhist liberation is available in any moment of awakening; it does not depend on a cosmic narrative.
- **Community vs. individual**: While both traditions have communal dimensions, Christian suffering is often interpreted within covenant community (the Church as body of Christ), whereas the Buddhist path, especially in the Theravada tradition, emphasizes individual practice (though Mahayana reintroduces communal compassion via the bodhisattva vow).

## Open Questions

- Is the Christian concept of "redemptive suffering" genuinely absent from Buddhism, or does the bodhisattva's voluntary suffering in Mahayana represent a structural parallel?
- How do the two traditions' different views on selfhood (soul vs. *anatta*) affect their practical pastoral responses to people in acute suffering?
- Can the Buddhist diagnostic framework (craving as the root of suffering) illuminate aspects of Christian theology — e.g., Augustine's notion of *concupiscentia* (disordered desire)?
- Post-Holocaust: does radical evil challenge both frameworks equally, or does theistic theodicy face a distinctive burden that non-theistic Buddhism does not?
